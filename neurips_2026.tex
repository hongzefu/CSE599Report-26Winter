\begin{abstract}
Long-horizon robotic manipulation is non-Markovian: counting events,
recovering object identity under occlusion, and reproducing demonstrated
motions all require retaining many seconds of visual history while the
policy runs at control rate within a fixed token budget. Recent benchmark
evidence~\citep{dai2026robomme} shows that perceptual memory representations
dominate motion-centric and time-sensitive tasks, but its concrete
instantiations --- uniform frame sampling and temporal-saliency-agnostic
token dropping --- are content-blind and degrade sharply as the memory
budget tightens, while symbolic / language-subgoal memory cannot adequately
represent low-level continuous motion. We introduce a robot-specialized
perceptual memory that decouples \emph{what} the robot saw from \emph{how}
things moved between observations: a CLIP-derivative keyframe selector with
delta-based per-keyframe TokenDrop carries sparse spatial evidence at
semantic transitions, and a learned MotionEncoder, pretrained on unlabeled
robot video by JEPA-style~\citep{assran2023ijepa} latent future-token
prediction, summarizes each short window into a single compact motion
token. The combined sequence is exposed to the $\pi_{0.5}$ backbone
through a fixed-budget memory interface compatible with all three
Memory-as-Context, -Modulator, and -Expert integration mechanisms used in
the MME-VLA suite~\citep{dai2026robomme}, enabling apples-to-apples
comparison against uniform-frame-sampling and token-dropping baselines at
matched memory budgets. Full quantitative evaluation on RoboMME is
\emph{in progress} (Sec.~\ref{sec:exp}).
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Open-world robotic manipulation is rarely Markovian. A household robot that
returns books to the right shelves must remember where each book originally
came from; a robot asked to ``press the button \emph{exactly} when the
moving cube reaches the target a specified number of times'' must
remember an event count and an event ordering; a robot reproducing a
demonstrated trajectory must replay motion patterns it observed seconds
earlier~\citep{dai2026robomme}. Modern vision-language-action (VLA) policies
run at control rate, on a transformer with a fixed input window, so the
central design question for memory-augmented manipulation is not
\emph{whether} to retain history but \emph{which projection of it} fits
inside that window without losing decision-relevant signal.

Two ends of the design spectrum have been explored, and each is
unsatisfactory. \emph{Symbolic} memory~\citep{bu2025univla,sridhar2025memer,geminiroboticsTeam2025gemini15}
discretizes history into language-based subgoals or natural-language plans;
this is cheap and human-interpretable, but recent controlled
evaluation~\citep{dai2026robomme} shows that language under-specifies
continuous motion and the resulting policies struggle on motion-centric
and precise visuomotor tasks even when given oracle subgoals. \emph{Perceptual}
memory keeps raw visual tokens~\citep{jang2025contextvla,koo2025hamlet,fang2025sam2act,shi2026memoryvla,physicalintelligence2026mem},
and is the empirically stronger family on motion-centric and long-horizon
tasks~\citep{dai2026robomme}; but the two perceptual instantiations
evaluated head-to-head in that study --- uniform frame sampling and
temporal-saliency-agnostic token dropping --- are content-blind. A uniform
sampler treats a critical contact event identically to a static-table
frame, and patch-level dropping that ignores temporal saliency removes the
global layout that timing- and counting-style tasks need. Both perceptual
variants degrade steeply as the memory budget shrinks, leaving open the
question of whether the perceptual family is structurally limited or only
its existing representatives are.

Robot videos have two complementary kinds of structure: a small number of
moments that genuinely matter (object pickup, contact, release, viewpoint
shift), and the relatively predictable motion that fills the gaps between
them. We argue the right perceptual memory should mirror that structure:
keep semantically chosen keyframes for \emph{what was seen and when}, and
learn a compact summary for \emph{how things moved between frames}. Our
motion summary is trained, JEPA-style~\citep{assran2023ijepa}, to predict
the latent SigLIP tokens of a future frame from those of the current frame
and a learnable motion code, so a single motion token is forced to encode
exactly the bridge information that any uniform-subsampling scheme throws
away.

We instantiate this idea in three pieces. First, a deterministic
CLIP-derivative keyframe detector selects frames at semantic transitions;
a delta-based per-keyframe TokenDrop then keeps only patches that changed
relative to the previous keyframe, plus their bounding rectangle for
spatial contiguity. Second, a small causal-transformer MotionEncoder
$f_\theta$ runs over fixed-stride short windows and emits one motion token
per window; it is pretrained on unlabeled robot video together with an
auxiliary MotionDiT decoder that regresses future SigLIP tokens, and the
decoder is discarded at deployment. Third, the resulting time-tagged
sequence is exposed as a fixed-budget memory state $\mathbf M_t$ through
the same Memory-as-Context, Memory-as-Modulator, and Memory-as-Expert
interfaces used by the MME-VLA suite of \citet{dai2026robomme} on the
$\pi_{0.5}$ backbone. The memory pipeline is task-agnostic and trained
once; only the VLA fine-tunes per task. Replacing the perceptual memory
module with our keyframe-plus-motion design is therefore a drop-in change
at every comparison point, so any observed effect is attributable to
memory \emph{content}, not \emph{injection}.

We evaluate this design on RoboMME~\citep{dai2026robomme} --- a long-horizon,
history-dependent benchmark spanning temporal, spatial, object, and
procedural memory tasks --- against uniform-frame-sampling and naive
token-drop perceptual baselines at matched memory budgets, under all
three integration mechanisms. Our hypothesis is that content-aware
keyframes plus a learned motion summary preserve more decision-relevant
signal per token than information-blind subsampling, particularly on
motion-centric and long-horizon tasks where symbolic memory cannot
represent continuous motion. Whether this hypothesis holds across budgets
and integration choices is the subject of Sec.~\ref{sec:exp}, where the
full quantitative evaluation is currently \emph{in progress}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item A robot-specialized perceptual memory that combines CLIP-scored
          semantic keyframes with delta-based TokenDrop and a learned
          MotionEncoder, decoupling sparse spatial evidence from compact
          short-range motion summaries inside a fixed token budget.
    \item A JEPA-style~\citep{assran2023ijepa} latent future-token-prediction
          objective with an auxiliary MotionDiT decoder that is discarded at
          deployment, used to pretrain the MotionEncoder on unlabeled robot
          video so each motion token is forced to encode exactly the
          information needed to bridge consecutive observations.
    \item A controlled evaluation protocol on
          RoboMME~\citep{dai2026robomme}: our memory plugs into the existing
          MME-VLA Memory-as-Context, -Modulator, and -Expert interfaces on
          the $\pi_{0.5}$ backbone, enabling apples-to-apples comparison
          against uniform-frame-sampling and naive token-drop baselines at
          matched memory budgets (full quantitative results: \emph{evaluation
          in progress}).
\end{itemize}


\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Memory-based Robotic Manipulation Models.}
History-dependent manipulation has motivated a rapidly growing family of
memory designs for VLA policies, but the design space remains fragmented.
The most direct treatment is to feed history in as additional context:
ContextVLA~\citep{jang2025contextvla} concatenates a sliding window of past
visual tokens to the transformer input, while
BC-RNN~\citep{mandlekar2021bcrnn} rolls history through a recurrent state.
Both extremes scale poorly --- the former pays linear attention cost in
horizon length, the latter compresses without selection. Recent state-space
policies, including RoboMamba~\citep{liu2024robomamba} and
MITL~\citep{mitl2025}, replace the RNN with Mamba-style
updates~\citep{gu2024mamba}, preserving constant-memory recurrence while
extending the effective context window.

A second line of work inserts an explicit \emph{memory bank} between the
encoder and the policy and reads from it on demand.
Hamlet~\citep{koo2025hamlet} and MemoryVLA~\citep{shi2026memoryvla} cache
past visual features together with auxiliary signals such as task
instructions or cognitive summaries, and retrieve the relevant entries at
each step; SAM2Act~\citep{fang2025sam2act} attaches a similar memory module
to a video segmentation backbone so that object identity and action heatmaps
remain stable across long horizons. MEM~\citep{physicalintelligence2026mem}
pushes this further by organizing embodied features at multiple temporal
resolutions, while BPP~\citep{sobol2026bpp} recasts long-context imitation
learning as the problem of selecting a small set of behaviorally relevant key
history frames. HistRISE~\citep{chen2025histrise} takes the structured-state
view to its extreme, tracking object-centric 3D points as a
non-differentiable symbolic memory.

A third, increasingly popular direction offloads memory entirely to a large
vision-language model. UniVLA~\citep{bu2025univla} simply prepends past
actions to the prompt, while MemER~\citep{sridhar2025memer},
Gemini-Robotics-1.5~\citep{geminiroboticsTeam2025gemini15}, and the
steerable $\pi_{0.7}$ generalist~\citep{physicalintelligence2026pi07} rely
on a VLM to produce language-based subgoals or natural-language plans that
condition a downstream policy. This keeps the policy itself short-context,
but introduces costly modular inference and breaks end-to-end optimization.
Taken together, these works establish that memory is essential, yet they
differ in backbone, in what is stored, and in how memory is integrated,
making head-to-head comparison difficult --- the gap our controlled study
targets, by training a family of memory-augmented VLAs on the same backbone
and evaluating diverse representations and integration mechanisms in a
unified setting.

\paragraph{Efficient Video Representation for Robotic Manipulation.}
Episodic robot videos are heavily redundant: adjacent frames differ only
in a few interaction-relevant pixels, and most patches encode static
background or the robot's own body. General video understanding addresses
this with token-level pruning and resampling ---
TimeChat-Online~\citep{yao2025timechat} shows that roughly 80\% of visual
tokens are naturally redundant in streaming video, and
VideoLLM-online~\citep{chen2024videollmonline} streams video into a language
model under an incremental token budget. In manipulation the constraint is
tighter still: a policy must run at control rate while keeping many seconds
of history within a fixed token window. Robotic methods therefore aggregate
frames at the encoder side --- ContextVLA~\citep{jang2025contextvla}
amortizes multi-frame context into a compact history bundle,
Hamlet~\citep{koo2025hamlet} turns a vanilla VLA into a history-aware policy
by switching its inputs at inference time, and long-context diffusion
policies~\citep{torne2025longcontext} supervise past-token prediction so that
the compressed history retains causally relevant information. A complementary
thread asks \emph{which} frames matter:
BPP~\citep{sobol2026bpp} explicitly selects key history frames for imitation
learning, and SAM2Act~\citep{fang2025sam2act} reuses a video segmentation
foundation model to anchor object identity across long horizons. We build on
these observations by combining TokenDropped keyframe patches with a
MotionEncoder summary, decoupling sparse visual evidence from high-frequency
motion structure and giving every memory variant the same fixed-size video
representation budget.

\subsection{Model Architecture}
\label{sec:arch}

Our policy is a memory-augmented vision-language-action (VLA) model built on
top of the $\pi_{0.5}$ backbone. We initialize from the pre-trained backbone
and fine-tune its experts end-to-end in Stage~2 (Sec.~\ref{sec:training}).
The only components kept frozen throughout both stages are the SigLIP2 visual
encoder and, after Stage~1, the MotionEncoder $f_\theta$.
We inject the per-episode memory sequence assembled in
Sec.~\ref{sec:memory} as additional context tokens.

\paragraph{$\pi_{0.5}$ backbone.}
At history timestep $t$, the policy receives language tokens
$\boldsymbol{\ell} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_\ell\times d}$ and multi-view image
tokens $\mathbf{o}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{N_o\times d}$ extracted by a frozen
SigLIP2 encoder. Following the $\pi_0$/$\pi_{0.5}$ multi-expert
design~\citep{black2024pi0,black2025pi05}, the backbone is composed of a
\emph{VLM expert} $E_{\mathrm{vlm}}$ that fuses language and vision and an
\emph{action expert} $E_{\mathrm{act}}$ that predicts an action chunk
conditioned on the VLM features and a denoising timestep $\tau$. Both
experts are $L$-layer transformers connected through blockwise causal
attention; the action expert is further conditioned on $\tau$ via
AdaLN-Zero modulation~\citep{peebles2023scalable}. We refer the reader to
the original papers for the exact equations. Unlike the original
$\pi_{0.5}$, which uses the VLM expert to also generate low-level language
commands, we keep the VLM as a pure feature extractor; this leaves a clean
interface at which memory can be injected.

\paragraph{Memory token block.}
To enable history-dependent reasoning, we augment the policy with an explicit
memory state $\mathbf{M}_t$:
\begin{equation}
  a_t \;\sim\; \pi\!\left(\cdot \;\middle|\; l,\; \mathbf{o}_t,\; \mathbf{M}_t\right),
  \label{eq:mem-policy}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{M}_t$ is the length-$B$ projection of the causal prefix
$\mathcal{M}_t$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:mem-causal}), containing only observations and
motion windows that have fully elapsed before step $t$.
For fair comparison across designs, we allocate a fixed memory token budget $B$
for all memory-based models: each representation produces at most $B$ tokens;
if fewer are available, the sequence is zero-padded.
Thus all memory variants share the same interface
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{M}_t \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{B\times d}.
  \label{eq:mem-interface}
\end{equation}
We expose the memory sequence $\mathcal{M}$ produced in
Sec.~\ref{sec:memory} to the backbone as this token block.
The two-token-kind structure of $\mathcal{M}$ — TokenDropped keyframe patches
and MotionEncoder motion tokens — is opaque to the backbone: from the policy's
perspective $\mathbf{M}_t$ is just a length-$B$ sequence of $d$-dimensional
vectors carrying time tags.

\paragraph{Three integration mechanisms.}
We systematically study three places at which $\mathbf{M}_t$ can enter the
backbone, trading parameter count, attention budget, and isolation of the
pretrained VLM features against each other.

\emph{(1) Memory-as-Context (MaC).} Memory tokens are concatenated with the
standard image and language inputs of the VLM expert and jointly processed.
This introduces no new parameters but lets memory compete with raw
observation tokens for the same attention budget inside $E_{\mathrm{vlm}}$.

\emph{(2) Memory-as-Modulator (MaM).} The action latent at each layer
cross-attends $\mathbf{M}_t$, and the result is mapped to per-feature
scale/shift parameters through a zero-initialized MLP composed with
AdaLN. The modulation is initialized to the identity, so fine-tuning starts
from the pretrained $\pi_{0.5}$ behavior. MaM adds $\sim\!80$M parameters
and leaves the VLM expert untouched.

\emph{(3) Memory-as-Expert (MaE).} A dedicated third expert
$E_{\mathrm{mem}}$ processes $\mathbf{M}_t$ through its own $L$ transformer
layers. Under an asymmetric blockwise causal mask, the VLM expert and
memory expert use self-attention only and do not attend each other, while
the action expert is the sole consumer that attends both as additional
context. This preserves the pretrained $\pi_{0.5}$ representations exactly
and allocates a separate transformer's worth of capacity ($\sim\!190$M
parameters) to memory processing.

\subsection{Memory Token Generation and Training}
\label{sec:memory}
% Scope: this subsection describes how the per-episode memory tokens consumed
% by the downstream VLA are produced and trained. The downstream injection
% mechanism (how the VLA attends to or interleaves these tokens) is described
% in Sec.~\ref{sec:arch}.

As the robot executes, the memory $\mathcal{M}_\tau$ grows online from the
history of observations accumulated prior to policy step $\tau$.  It is a
sparse, time-aligned sequence of two complementary token kinds:
(i) \emph{keyframe tokens}, which anchor the scene at semantic transitions,
and (ii) \emph{motion tokens}, fixed-stride summaries of short-range
visual change produced by a learned MotionEncoder. Both kinds are encoded
strictly from already-elapsed frames: keyframe tokens are committed as soon
as a keyframe is detected, and a motion token $m_t$ over window
$[t,\,t\!+\!\Delta]$ becomes available only after step $t\!+\!\Delta$ has
passed (Eq.~\ref{eq:mem-causal}). All encoding is performed by frozen
perception backbones independently of the VLA; no gradient flows back into
these encoders during VLA training.

\paragraph{Keyframe detection.}
Let $\{I_t\}_{t \geq 0}\!\subset\!\mathbb{R}^{3\times H\times W}$ denote the sequence of RGB frames observed during execution.
Keyframes mark moments of meaningful scene change (object pickup,
contact events, viewpoint shifts). Each frame is scored by the smoothed
discrete derivative of a frozen CLIP encoder $\Phi_{\mathrm{CLIP}}$:
\begin{equation}
  d_t \;=\; \big\| \Phi_{\mathrm{CLIP}}(I_t) - \Phi_{\mathrm{CLIP}}(I_{t-1}) \big\|_2,
  \qquad
  \tilde d_t \;=\; (k_{\mathrm{rbf}} * d)_t,
\end{equation}
where $k_{\mathrm{rbf}}$ is a small RBF kernel that suppresses frame-rate
jitter. Keyframes are local maxima of $\tilde d_t$ exceeding a threshold
$\tau$, with the first frame $t=0$ always retained as an anchor. The
detector is purely deterministic preprocessing and has no learnable
parameters; $\tau$ is a single hyperparameter chosen so that the average
inter-keyframe gap is $\sim\!32$ frames.

\paragraph{Keyframe TokenDrop.}
Storing the full $S$-token grid for every keyframe is wasteful: most of
the image (background, robot base, table) repeats across keyframes. For
every keyframe $k_i$ with $i\!>\!0$ we retain only the patches that
actually changed relative to its \emph{previous} keyframe $k_{i-1}$.
Concretely, on the $16\!\times\!16$ patch grid we mark a patch as
\emph{significant} if its mean per-pixel absolute difference between
$I_{k_i}$ and $I_{k_{i-1}}$ exceeds $\delta$, and we keep the union of
all significant patches together with the patches lying inside their
axis-aligned bounding rectangle (the latter preserves spatial contiguity
for downstream attention). The first keyframe $k_0$ is always kept in
full ($S$ tokens); each subsequent keyframe contributes
$|\mathcal{P}_i|\!\le\!S$ tokens. TokenDrop is also parameter-free.

\paragraph{Motion tokens.}
Let $\Phi_{\mathrm{SigLIP}}\!:\!\mathbb{R}^{3\times H\times W}\!\to\!\mathbb{R}^{S\times E}$ ($S\!=\!256$ patches, $E\!=\!1152$) be the frozen SigLIP tokenizer, and let $x_t \!=\! \Phi_{\mathrm{SigLIP}}(I_t)\in\mathbb{R}^{S\times E}$; $\{s_t\}$ denotes the proprioceptive state sequence.
Independently of keyframe placement, we tile the video with non-overlapping
windows of fixed stride $\Delta$ (we use $\Delta\!=\!8$). For each window
$[t,\, t\!+\!\Delta]$ a trainable \emph{MotionEncoder}
$f_\theta$ compresses the visual change into $M$ latent tokens
$m_{t}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{M\times D_m}$:
\begin{equation}
  m_t \;=\; f_\theta\!\left(\, x_t,\; x_{t+\Delta},\; \Delta s_t \,\right),
  \qquad \Delta s_t = s_{t+\Delta} - s_t.
\end{equation}
Internally, $f_\theta$ (i) computes the patchwise visual delta
$\Delta x = x_{t+\Delta} - x_t \in \mathbb{R}^{S\times E}$, (ii) spatially
average-pools $\Delta x$ to a single global delta token in
$\mathbb{R}^{1\times E}$ and projects it to the encoder width $H$,
(iii) projects $\Delta s_t$ to $H$ and adds it to the pooled delta token
(this is the only place proprioception enters the memory pipeline),
(iv) appends $M$ learned read-out queries at the tail, and
(v) runs a small causal transformer (RMSNorm, 1D-RoPE causal
self-attention, SwiGLU FFN) so that each query attends to the fused
delta token before it. The last $M$ positions are projected to motion
dimension $D_m$ and constitute $m_t$.
Since $m_t$ requires both endpoints of window $[t,\,t\!+\!\Delta]$, it becomes
causally available only at step $t\!+\!\Delta$.

\paragraph{Memory sequence assembly.}
The final memory for an episode is the time-ordered concatenation
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{M} \;=\; \big[\, x_{k_0}^{\mathrm{full}},\; m_{0},\; m_{\Delta},\; \dots,\; x_{k_i}^{\mathrm{TD}},\; \dots \,\big],
\end{equation}
where each entry carries its source timestamp as a positional tag.
At policy step $\tau$, the policy receives the causally valid prefix
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{M}_\tau \;=\;
    \bigl\{\,x_{k_j}^{\cdot} : k_j \leq \tau\,\bigr\}
    \;\cup\;
    \bigl\{\,m_t : t + \Delta \leq \tau\,\bigr\},
  \label{eq:mem-causal}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ keyframe tokens whose frame index is at most $\tau$, and motion tokens
whose entire window $[t,\,t\!+\!\Delta]$ has elapsed by $\tau$.
The sequence $\mathcal{M}_\tau$ grows monotonically: at each new step the
pipeline appends newly completed tokens without recomputing earlier ones.
Keyframe selection and TokenDrop are training-free; the only learnable
parameters in the memory pipeline are those of the MotionEncoder
$f_\theta$, which is pre-trained in Stage~1 of Sec.~\ref{sec:objective}
and then frozen. The trained $f_\theta$, together with the
parameter-free keyframe and TokenDrop modules, is what ultimately writes
into $\mathcal{M}$.

\subsection{Training Objective}
\label{sec:objective}

The full system has two distinct loss surfaces, optimized in two stages:
a latent future-prediction loss $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$ that trains
the MotionEncoder $f_\theta$ of Sec.~\ref{sec:memory}, and a flow-matching
loss $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FM}}$ that trains the VLA backbone together
with the integration module.

\paragraph{Latent future-prediction loss for $f_\theta$.}
We train $f_\theta$ jointly with an auxiliary \emph{MotionDiT} decoder
$g_\phi$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:training}): a conditional-diffusion decoder that regresses the SigLIP
tokens of the future frame from the current tokens and the motion code.
Given $(x_t, x_{t+\Delta})$, we sample a diffusion-style timestep
$k\!\sim\!\mathcal{U}\{1,\dots,K\}$ and ask $g_\phi$ to predict the
(token-normalized) future tokens from the current tokens, the motion
code, and $k$:
\begin{equation}
  \hat x_{t+\Delta}^{(k)} \;=\; g_\phi\!\left(\, x_t,\; m_t,\; k \,\right),
  \qquad m_t = f_\theta(x_t, x_{t+\Delta}, \Delta s_t),
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}} \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}_{t,\,k}\!\left[\,
    \big\| \hat x_{t+\Delta}^{(k)} \;-\; \mathrm{TokenNorm}(x_{t+\Delta}) \big\|_2^2
  \,\right].
\end{equation}
$\mathrm{TokenNorm}$ is a per-feature standardization applied to the
target SigLIP tokens to stabilize the regression target. $g_\phi$ is a
small DiT with AdaLN-zero timestep modulation and a tied gated
input/output projection initialized to zero, so at $k\!=\!0$ the decoder
acts as a normalized identity copy of $x_t$ and only learns residual
corrections driven by $m_t$. This zero-initialized residual head is the
sole reason the loss is stable when $f_\theta$ is randomly initialized:
gradients flow through $g_\phi$ into $f_\theta$ only via the correction
term, forcing $m_t$ to encode exactly the information needed to bridge
$x_t \to x_{t+\Delta}$. Crucially, $g_\phi$ exists only during
pre-training: at deployment the robot has already executed both endpoints
of every motion window, so memory tokens are produced by a single forward
pass through $\Phi_{\mathrm{SigLIP}}$ and $f_\theta$; no decoding is
performed and $g_\phi$ is discarded.

    The MotionEncoder $f_\theta$ and the auxiliary MotionDiT $g_\phi$ are

    Both modules receive SigLIP features from the current frame $I_t$;
    $f_\theta$ additionally takes the future frame $I_{t+\Delta}$ and the

\paragraph{Flow-matching action loss.}
Following $\pi_{0.5}$, the policy predicts an $H$-step action chunk
$\mathbf{A}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{H\times D}$ via conditional flow matching:
the action expert parameterizes a velocity field
$\mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{A}_t^\tau,\tau,\mathbf{h}_t)$ trained to match
the noise-to-data transport direction,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FM}} \;=\;
  \mathbb{E}\!\left[\,
    \big\| \mathbf{v}_\theta\!\big(\mathbf{A}_t^\tau,\tau,\mathbf{h}_t\big)
    \;-\; \big(\mathbf{A}_t^{\mathrm{noise}} - \mathbf{A}_t^{\mathrm{data}}\big) \big\|_2^2
  \,\right],
  \label{eq:fm-loss}
\end{equation}
where the context features $\mathbf{h}_t$ are the layer-$L$ outputs of the
integrated backbone and therefore depend on the chosen integration
mechanism (MaC, MaM, or MaE). At inference the action chunk is generated
by integrating $\mathbf{v}_\theta$ from $\tau\!=\!1$ to $\tau\!=\!0$ through
a small number of Euler steps.

\paragraph{Two-stage training.}
The two losses are optimized sequentially, not jointly, because the memory
pipeline encodes a task-agnostic visual-dynamics prior whereas the
integration module is task-specific.

\emph{Stage 1: Memory-pipeline pre-training.} On unlabeled robot videos we
jointly train the MotionEncoder $f_\theta$ and the auxiliary MotionDiT
decoder $g_\phi$ to minimize $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$. The SigLIP and
CLIP backbones, the keyframe detector, and the TokenDrop module are
training-free throughout. After Stage~1, $g_\phi$ is discarded and
$f_\theta$ is frozen.

\emph{Stage 2: VLA fine-tuning.} For every demonstration episode we
pre-compute and cache the full memory sequence $\mathcal{M}$ using the
frozen pipeline of Stage~1, together with SigLIP2 visual tokens. At each
training step $t$ the causally valid prefix
$\mathcal{M}_t$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:mem-causal}) is projected to
$\mathbf{M}_t \in \mathbb{R}^{B\times d}$ and fed to the chosen integration
module; the VLM expert, action expert, and integration module are trained
end-to-end on $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FM}}$. No proprioceptive state enters
the backbone in Stage~2 --- proprioception only participates in
Sec.~\ref{sec:memory} when forming motion tokens.

This decoupling is what makes the same memory pipeline reusable across all
three integration variants and across downstream tasks: Stage~1 is run
once; Stage~2 is repeated for each (task, integration) configuration.

\paragraph{Memory budget and compression.}
We cap the keyframe-token memory at $B\!=\!2000$ tokens of width
$d\!=\!1024$; the MotionEncoder contributes up to $125$ motion tokens

when shorter.  Compared with the naive flattened representation of
$T\!\cdot\!S$ patch tokens per episode, this is a ${\sim}\,250\times$

keyframe anchors (the first keyframe contributes a full $S\!=\!256$
tokens) and short-range dynamics ($M\!=\!1$ motion token per
$\Delta\!=\!16$-frame window).

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:exp}

Our evaluation is structured around three research questions.
\textbf{RQ1 (memory content).} Under a fixed token budget and a fixed
integration mechanism, do content-aware perceptual memories (CLIP keyframes,
TokenDrop, learned motion tokens) preserve more decision-relevant signal
than the content-blind subsampling baselines used in
MME-VLA~\citep{dai2026robomme}?
\textbf{RQ2 (budget sensitivity).} How does each memory variant degrade as
the memory budget $B$ shrinks --- gracefully, or with a cliff?
\textbf{RQ3 (integration).} Among Memory-as-Context, Memory-as-Modulation,
and Memory-as-Expert, which integration mechanism best exploits a given
memory representation?
At the time of submission, the budget-sensitivity component of RQ2 has
finished for both content-blind baselines, while the keyframe-based
configurations and the cross-backbone study addressing RQ1 and RQ3 are
still running; we report what is currently available and explicitly mark
all unfinished cells as \emph{ongoing} (Sec.~\ref{sec:main-results}).

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Benchmark.}
We evaluate on RoboMME~\citep{dai2026robomme}, a long-horizon,
history-dependent manipulation suite that explicitly tests four families of
memory: \emph{temporal} (event counting and ordering), \emph{spatial}
(layout and viewpoint persistence), \emph{object} (identity under
occlusion), and \emph{procedural} (reproducing demonstrated motion).
RoboMME provides matched train/eval splits, a unified scoring protocol, and
the MME-VLA reference policies, which makes it the natural testbed for a
controlled comparison of memory \emph{content} at matched memory budgets.

\paragraph{Baselines and our model.}
All configurations share the $\pi_{0.5}$ backbone and the
\emph{Memory-as-Modulation} (MaM) integration mechanism described in
Sec.~\ref{sec:arch}, so any difference is attributable to memory content.
The full set of compared methods is enumerated in
Sec.~\ref{sec:main-results} (\textit{Baselines} paragraph): two
content-blind baselines (\emph{Even Frame Sampling} and \emph{Even
TokenDropping}) swept over five token budgets
$B\!\in\!\{1024,512,256,128,64\}$, two keyframe-based ablations
(\emph{Clip-Keyframe} as an upper-bound configuration with $\sim\!8000{+}$
tokens, and \emph{Clip-Keyframe + TokenDrop} at the matched budget of
$2000$), our full \emph{Clip-Keyframe + TokenDrop + Motion} memory at
$2000{+}125$, and an additional cross-backbone study with OpenVLA and
Qwen2.5-VL.

\paragraph{Metric.}
Following RoboMME~\citep{dai2026robomme}, we report task Success Rate
(\%) averaged over the benchmark's evaluation episodes; higher is better.

\paragraph{Training and inference.}
We follow the two-stage recipe of Sec.~\ref{sec:objective}: Stage~1
pre-trains the MotionEncoder $f_\theta$ once on unlabeled robot video with
$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{pred}}$, after which $f_\theta$ is frozen and reused
across every (memory, integration, backbone) configuration; Stage~2
fine-tunes the policy with $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{FM}}$ for $80$k steps
under the optimizer and hardware setup described in Sec.~\ref{sec:impl}.
At inference the memory pipeline runs strictly causally
(Eq.~\ref{eq:mem-causal}): only motion windows whose right endpoint has
elapsed before the current step contribute to $\mathbf{M}_t$.

\subsection{Main Results}
\label{sec:main-results}

This subsection collects the results currently available against the
memory-content question (RQ1) and the budget-sensitivity question (RQ2).
The completed runs cover the two content-blind perceptual baselines
(\emph{Even Frame Sampling} and \emph{Even TokenDropping}) across the
full budget sweep $B\!\in\!\{1024,512,256,128,64\}$ under MaM; the
keyframe-based configurations and the cross-backbone study are still
running and appear as \emph{ongoing} entries in Tab.~\ref{tab:main}.

\paragraph{Baselines.}
We compare our method against five reference configurations and an
extra backbone study, all instantiated under the
\emph{Memory-as-Modulation} (MaM) integration mechanism so that
differences are attributable to memory \emph{content} rather than
\emph{injection}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{MME-VLA Clip-Keyframe} retains every $16{\times}16$
        token of the selected keyframes (\S\ref{sec:memory})
        without compression, giving an upper-bound configuration of
        $\sim\!8000{+}$ tokens.
  \item \textbf{MME-VLA Clip-Keyframe + TokenDrop} adds the
        temporal-saliency token-dropping policy on top, compressing the
        memory to a fixed budget of $2000$ tokens.
  \item \textbf{MME-VLA Clip-Keyframe + TokenDrop + Motion (Ours)}
        further appends $125$ motion tokens that summarise short-range
        dynamics, yielding a total budget of $2000{+}125$.
  \item \textbf{MME-VLA Even Frame Sampling} is the naive frame-sampling
        baseline (\S\ref{sec:memory}, FrameSamp): uniformly
        spaced frames with pooled tokens, swept over budgets
        $\{1024, 512, 256, 128, 64\}$ to characterise how purely
        temporal subsampling degrades with shrinking memory.
  \item \textbf{MME-VLA Even TokenDropping} is the naive token-dropping
        baseline that ignores temporal-saliency scoring, swept over the
        same five budgets to isolate the value of the
        importance-weighted selection used by our method.
  \item \textbf{Other backbones (OpenVLA, Qwen2.5-VL)} swap the
        $\pi_{0.5}$ backbone for two alternative VLA backbones with the
        same memory pipeline, probing how transferable the proposed
        memory design is across model families.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Findings on the content-blind baselines.}
Two observations follow directly from the completed runs in
Tab.~\ref{tab:main}.
\emph{(i) Frame sampling consistently dominates token dropping at matched
budget.} At every budget $B$, \emph{Even Frame Sampling} outperforms
\emph{Even TokenDropping}: the gap is $+5.7$ pt at $B\!=\!1024$ and widens
to $+12.3$ pt at $B\!=\!64$. Keeping a few full frames is therefore a
strictly stronger memory than retaining the same number of arbitrarily
selected patches, even when the patch budget is comparatively generous.
\emph{(ii) The two baselines degrade differently as the budget shrinks.}
\emph{Even Frame Sampling} loses $15.45$ pt across the full sweep
($45.87\!\to\!30.42$), with monotone but accelerating per-halving drops
($1.36$, $1.61$, $6.36$, $6.12$ pt);
\emph{Even TokenDropping} loses $22.06$ pt across the same sweep
($40.17\!\to\!18.11$), and exhibits a clear cliff between $B\!=\!128$ and
$B\!=\!64$ ($32.76\!\to\!18.11$, a $14.65$ pt single-step drop).
This asymmetry is consistent with our motivation
(Sec.~\ref{sec:intro}): saliency-agnostic patch removal eventually deletes
patches that anchor global layout, after which the policy cannot
reconstruct the scene from what remains, while uniform frame subsampling
loses temporal resolution more gracefully because each retained frame still
carries a complete spatial layout. Whether content-aware selection (CLIP
keyframes, TokenDrop, motion tokens) closes this gap and surpasses
\emph{Even Frame Sampling} at matched budgets is the central remaining
question; those rows are still running and will replace the
\emph{ongoing} cells in Tab.~\ref{tab:main}.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
\label{sec:ablation}

A full ablation matrix over the components of our memory pipeline and the
three integration mechanisms is currently in progress, sharing the same
backbone, optimizer, and budget conventions as Sec.~\ref{sec:main-results}.
The planned ablation axes are:
(i) keyframe selection (CLIP-derivative vs.\ uniform) at matched budget;
(ii) per-keyframe TokenDrop on/off, isolating the contribution of
delta-based patch retention beyond keyframe selection;
(iii) MotionEncoder tokens on/off, isolating the contribution of the
JEPA-style learned motion summary;
(iv) integration mechanism, swapping MaM for Memory-as-Context and
Memory-as-Expert on the same memory tokens, addressing RQ3; and
(v) backbone, swapping $\pi_{0.5}$ for OpenVLA and Qwen2.5-VL with the
memory pipeline held fixed, probing transferability.
We do not report partial ablation numbers here to avoid over-interpreting
unfinished sweeps.

\subsection{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\paragraph{Two qualitatively different degradation modes.}
Reading the completed entries of Tab.~\ref{tab:main} as two budget-success
curves makes the structural difference between the baselines explicit.
\emph{Even Frame Sampling} stays nearly flat over the looser half of the
sweep ($1.36$ and $1.61$ pt drops at $1024\!\to\!512$ and $512\!\to\!256$)
before transitioning into a steeper but still bounded regime in the tighter
half ($6.36$ and $6.12$ pt at $256\!\to\!128$ and $128\!\to\!64$).
\emph{Even TokenDropping} starts $5.7$ pt below at the loosest budget,
tracks comparable per-halving losses through $B\!=\!256$ ($2.15$, $1.66$,
and $3.60$ pt), and then collapses by $14.65$ pt between $B\!=\!128$ and
$B\!=\!64$. We read the cliff as evidence that content-blind patch removal
crosses a representational threshold: below this budget the surviving
patches no longer anchor enough global structure for the action expert to
recover the scene, whereas frame-level subsampling continues to deliver
intact-but-coarser temporal slices. From a memory-design perspective, this
is the empirical signature that motivates content-aware selection ---
deciding \emph{which} patches to keep is most consequential exactly where
the budget is tightest, which is also where motion-centric and
counting-style RoboMME tasks place their hardest queries.

\paragraph{Caveats.}
The analysis above is restricted to the two completed sweeps; we do not
extrapolate to the keyframe-based or cross-backbone configurations whose
runs are still in progress. We will revisit the per-task breakdown
(temporal / spatial / object / procedural) and report failure cases in the
final version, once the \emph{ongoing} cells of Tab.~\ref{tab:main} are
filled in.


\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a robot-specialized perceptual memory that decouples
\emph{what was seen and when} from \emph{how things moved between
observations}. CLIP-derivative keyframe detection picks frames at semantic
transitions, a delta-based per-keyframe TokenDrop retains only the patches
that change relative to the previous keyframe, and a small MotionEncoder
$f_\theta$, pretrained JEPA-style with an auxiliary MotionDiT decoder that
is discarded at deployment, summarizes each short window into a single
motion token. The resulting fixed-budget memory is exposed to the
$\pi_{0.5}$ backbone through three interchangeable interfaces ---
Memory-as-Context, Memory-as-Modulation, and Memory-as-Expert --- so that
content-blind subsampling baselines and our content-aware design can be
compared head-to-head on RoboMME at matched memory budgets and matched
integration. The completed budget sweeps already establish that the two
content-blind perceptual variants of MME-VLA degrade in qualitatively
different ways: \emph{Even Frame Sampling} loses $15.45$ pt nearly
linearly across $B\!\in\![1024,64]$, while \emph{Even TokenDropping} loses
$22.06$ pt with a sharp $14.65$-pt cliff at $B\!=\!64$, indicating that
which patches are retained matters most precisely when the budget is
smallest. This is the controlled signature our keyframe-and-motion design
is meant to exploit.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
Our quantitative evaluation is incomplete: at the time of submission,
the \emph{Clip-Keyframe}, \emph{Clip-Keyframe + TokenDrop}, and
\emph{Clip-Keyframe + TokenDrop + Motion (Ours)} configurations under MaM,
the comparison across the three integration mechanisms (MaC / MaM / MaE)
called for by RQ3, and the cross-backbone study with OpenVLA and
Qwen2.5-VL are all still running. The analysis we present here is
therefore restricted to the two completed content-blind baselines; a
per-task breakdown across RoboMME's temporal / spatial / object /
procedural categories, formal ablations of TokenDrop and motion tokens,
and qualitative failure cases will be reported once those runs finish.
Beyond completing the present evaluation, three directions look
particularly worth pursuing: (i) online or streaming updates to
$\mathbf{M}_t$ that recompute keyframe assignments as the episode evolves;
(ii) hybrid memories that combine our perceptual tokens with the
language-subgoal memories used by recent VLM-driven
policies~\citep{sridhar2025memer,geminiroboticsTeam2025gemini15}, since the
two families capture complementary aspects of history; and (iii)
validation on real hardware beyond the RoboMME simulation suite, where
sensor noise and timing jitter will further stress the keyframe detector
and the MotionEncoder.
